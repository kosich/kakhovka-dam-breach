## Preface

On February 24th, 2022 russia occupied Kakhovka dam.  
On June 6th, 2023 Kakhovka dam was breached.

## Summary

Currently widely accepted version of dam breach is that it was blown up. In this document I want to show that our evidence of explosion is not sufficient enough, and that there are signs of possible progressing riverbed [scouring](#what-is-that-scouring-that-you-keep-mentioning) for at least 38 days before the breach. Which could've led to dam collapse on June 6th. Mismanaging critical infrastructure can be as dangerous as putting explosives on it, and there’s good evidence that the dam was unintentionally or intentionally ill-maintained during the russian occupation.

To understand what happened on June 6th, 2023 we need to understand how the dam works and what was happening at the dam before the tragedy.

Water flows from heigher levels (reservoir) to lower levels. The energy water carries turns turbines at the Hydro Power Plant (HPP, on the right). If there's excess water — it is drained through sluice gates, that stand on a gravity dam (weir). Water drained though sluice gates still carries energy, which should be dissipated. Protective riverbed layer downstream from the dam helps slow down the water and protect sandy river floor from erosion.

Now, since late 2016 and till russian occupation of the dam in Feb 2022, there was only one visible from satellites water drainage in Spring floods of 2018, which lasted for at most 18 days:

Gate opening happened when HPP turbines didn’t spend enough water during Spring floods. Opening of the sluice gates is not a rare thing, but doesn’t happen often either. In this case gate opening was done in phases, according to existing water load from the Kakhovka reservoir. This decision was ratified by a specialized committee, and even got to the UkrHydroEnergo news: [gates opening in 2018](#gates-opening-in-2018). Note that the gates were opened in a symmetrical to each other and to the HPP layout. Meaning that right-bank and left-bank gates are opened symmetrically. First [2 + HPP](#10-04-2018-two-opened-hpp), then [2 + 2 + HPP](#11-04-2018-4-opened-hpp), then [4 + 2 + HPP](#14-april-2018-6-opened-hpp).

Since russian occupation of the dam, there were two massive drainages through the gates:

**First drainage** lasted at least 45 days in May-June 2022:

**Second drainage** was a 8 months of almost continuous drainage from October till dam breach in June, last 7 months of which (235 days) is uninterrupted drainage through gates on just one side, near the HPP. On the same side, where the June 2023 breach will happen:

During this period the drainage happens only on one side of the river, near the HPP. There’s no symmetry in the opened gates. There’s no open-closed-open pattern of the gates that we often see [during drainages](#open-gates-2013). Gates #1, #3, #5, #6, #7 are opened together with HPP gates for 235 days straight.

This continuous uncontrolled drainage led to critical Kakhovka reservoir water levels.

First to [record low levels](#24-march-2023) in winter time (when Dnipro has less water income), and then to [record high levels](#16-april-4-may-2023-first-signs-of-overtopping) during spring floods (when rains and melting snow bring a lot of water to Dnipro). The five constantly opened gates first gave too much water in Winter, and then the same five opened gates were not enough for the Spring floods.

Constant drainage and big water level difference in Spring likely led to river bed scouring, first indication of which we see on [28 April 2023 — part of the wall separating HPP & dam breaks](#28-april-2023-part-of-the-wall-separating-hpp-dam-breaks):

River bed scouring actually already happened when the dam was under construction in the 1950's: [K-Dam river bed was already scoured once](#k-dam-river-bed-was-already-scoured-washed-out-once-in-1950-s). A flood occurred during the construction of the dam. This forced engineers to drain water through gates on one side of the dam, while water level downstream of the dam was 2 meters below normal. That led to river bed scouring / washout just downstream from rysberma for 9m deep (rysberma is a protective riverbed layer downstream from the dam, see [blueprints](#blueprints) for more info).

So we had riverbed scouring in the 1950's, in a similar situation, some 100+ meters downstream (disclaimer: I bet it got fixed then, I reference it only as a similar case). Then we have the separator wall collapse on [28 April 2023](#28-april-2023-part-of-the-wall-separating-hpp-dam-breaks), 38 days before the breach, some 100 meters downstream from the barrage. Then we have the bridge foundations collapse on [1-2 and 2-3 of June 2023](#1-2-june-2-3-june-2023-overtopping-fall-of-the-s-bridge), four to three days before the catastrophe, right at the base of the dam.

Progressing upstream river bed scouring could undermine the basis of the weir and the HPP structures. Which could cause them to collapse under their own weight and water pressure from the reservoir, leading to the dam breach on [6th of June, 2023](#6-june-2023-breach):

## What is that scouring that you keep mentioning?

<Image src={require('./assets/image107-0d483555.png').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

> Hydrodynamic scour is the removal of sediment such as silt, sand and gravel from around the base of obstructions to the flow in the sea, rivers and canals. Scour, caused by fast flowing water, can carve out scour holes, compromising the integrity of a structure.  
> <Subtle>Source: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamic\_scour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamic_scour)</Subtle>

<Image src={require('./assets/image79-953881d2.png').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

> It is a notable cause of [bridge failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge_scour) and a problem with most marine structures supported by the seabed in areas of significant [tidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_scour) and ocean current.  
>
> It has been estimated that 60% of all bridge failures result from scour and other hydraulic-related causes.  
> <Subtle>Source: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge\_scour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridge_scour)</Subtle>

According to [these posts by UkrHydroElectro](#gates-opening-in-2018) from 2018 — one gate roughly drains 100 cubic meters of water per second. Let’s take that at least since 02 January 2023 three gates are fully opened and two are half-opened. And let’s ignore overdrainage and overtopping for simplicity, we’ll say that they cancel each other out. That means we have roughly 400 cubic meters of water drained each second. Giving us roughly 5\_356\_800\_000 cubic meters drained, if only counting the last 5 months before the breach. And we’re even not counting the opened gates on the HPP.

## Does it mean that the dam broke on its own?

No. If someone leaves a stove lit, and the house catches fire — we don’t say “the house burned down on its own”. It is an arson.

Albeit being monumental and robust, dams require professional maintenance and precise manipulation. And they can fail for a variety of reasons: [https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dam\_failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dam_failure), including poor maintenance.

## What about the evidence of an explosion?

Well, currently evidence of an explosion is unverifiable and contradictory. It doesn’t mean that there were no explosions. It only means that our evidence for the explosion demolition version is not enough. See this section for more: [Explosion theory evidence and critics](#explosion-theory-evidence-and-critics)

## So russia did it by explosives or by ill-maintenance, does it really matter which way?

It doesn’t matter for russia — they are guilty either way and should face the consequences. But for the world it is important to learn how exactly the breach happened, and how we can prevent similar disasters in the future.

If some terrorists want to blow up a hazardous infrastructure that they captured — there’s little we can do to prevent that. We had to act before, not letting terrorists into the place. But if this disaster happened by knowing or unknowing mis-maintenance — then the world could foresee this catastrophe and intervene with social, political, economic and raw force months before the breach. Actually, the OSINT community has been warning the world about possible catastrophe, but our existing international institutions could not or would not prevent the disaster.

If this evidence of scouring / washout indeed was sufficient to cause the dam breach — then russia should’ve noticed the breaking structures at least 38 days before the breach. Thus before we prove explosion or erosion, we already have a case of criminal inaction.

May I also remind you: aside from many suffering cities and villages, russia also currently occupies the  [Zaporizhzhia Nuclear Power Plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zaporizhzhia_Nuclear_Power_Plant).

##### My speculations on how it happened and what this means

From the tip of the wall till the sluice gates there’s about 100 - 120 meters:

<Image src={require('./assets/image124-231af5a0.jpg').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

Thus we can deduce foundation under that piece that broke off:

<Image src={require('./assets/image106-618c061e.jpg').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

Looks like that tip that broke off was standing on or near the [rysberma](https://uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A0%D0%B8%D1%81%D0%B1%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%BC%D0%B0) or riprap (#5 above). Riprap is a layer of rocks and concrete, that lies right after the 4m thick concrete apron (#4). Riprap protects riverbottom from scouring, breaks and slows down the stream to normalize it with the rest of the water. It is claimed that during Kakhovka dam construction water levels could not be managed properly during floods. Because of that water drainage happened in extreme conditions, which lead to riverbed scouring just downstream from rysberma. [K-Dam river bed was already scoured once](#k-dam-river-bed-was-already-scoured-washed-out-once-in-1950-s)

After that wall tip broke off we can say that the riprap was damaged and the riverbed was prone to further scouring.

Further water drainage through gates #1, #3, #5-7 and probably HPP gates would further erode the foundation of the wall, leading to the foundations of the s-bridge that would fall on [June 1-2-3](#1-2-june-2-3-june-2023-overtopping-fall-of-the-s-bridge).

##### K-Dam river bed was already scoured / washed out once in 1950s

In 1950-s a flood occurred during the construction of the dam. This forced engineers to drain water through gates on one side of the dam, while water level downstream of the dam was 2 meters below normal — that led to river bed scouring / washout downstream from [rysberma](https://uk.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D0%A0%D0%B8%D1%81%D0%B1%D0%B5%D1%80%D0%BC%D0%B0) (riprap) for 9m deep.

## Explosion theory evidence and critics

### NYT article on barrage explosive demolition

<Subtle>
  Article: [https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2023/06/16/world/europe/ukraine-kakhovka-dam-collapse.html](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2023/06/16/world/europe/ukraine-kakhovka-dam-collapse.html)  
  Tweet: [https://twitter.com/nytimes/status/1670441253094260737](https://twitter.com/nytimes/status/1670441253094260737) 
</Subtle>

My concerns regarding the article:

#### The article ignores that there are signs of erosion

NYT experts themselves say:

> Neither this previous damage, nor the pressure caused by the high water level or the static position of the cranes is likely to have caused the collapse of the dam’s concrete foundation, experts said, unless the concrete was of low quality and already prone to deteriorate. The large flows would also be insufficient to undermine the dam’s foundation **unless, for some reason, the concrete apron — the downstream cover placed over the river bottom — contained flaws** or the soil was much softer than accounted for in the design.

But they don't know, or don't go further discussing signs of **concrete apron** erosion. While we know that separator wall break and s-bridge fall might indicate protective layer damage. And we know that the soil downstream the dam was already once eroded in 1950s.

#### Submerged part of the weir

<figure>
  <Image src={require('./assets/image129-ca1a9b93.jpg').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

  <Image src={require('./assets/image57-cd1bea49.jpg').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />
  <figcaption>
    Photos and illustrations from the article

    <Subtle>Source of the video NYT article uses: [https://t.me/milinfolive/102188](https://t.me/milinfolive/102188)</Subtle>
  </figcaption>
</figure>

NYT article claims that since "left" part of the weir is above the water, and on the "right" it is submerged — that means that the part on the right was blown up.

Albeit we clearly see on the photos and illustrations in the article, that the weir is bended (broken) downwards. Not downstream, downwards, into the soil under the weir. And if the soil foundation of the dam was intact — there'd be nowhere to bend to.

<figure>
  <Image src={require('./assets/image28-292ca36d.jpg').default} alt="" placeholder="blur" />

  <figcaption>
    Here we can see a V-shaped break of the weir
  </figcaption>
</figure>

If the dam was blown up — it caused change in waterflow, which caused water to scour soil foundation, which caused the weir to bend under it's own weight. Or it could start with upstream progressing scour, creating a hole under the weir, which caused it break under it's own weight.

Sidenote: another perspective to show that the weir bended downwards, not downstream:

And we know that after the initial breach — there was some scouring under the constructions:

> **[Over/\_\\Drive](https://twitter.com/DraftnDepth) [@DraftnDepth](https://twitter.com/DraftnDepth)**

Thus this bend that NYT article highlights doesn't proove anything. There had to be soil erosion, before the weir could break down.
